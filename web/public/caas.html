Coworker,
as a service.

The paradigm behind Beckett, as we mean it at The Commons. Spoiler: it's not a chatbot with a job title.

01 · the lineage

Software kept almost helping.

SaaS gave you tools; you still did the work. Chatbots gave you answers; you still did the work. Copilots gave you suggestions; you were still the one driving. CaaS is the next honest step: you hand over the outcome, not the keystrokes.

02 · delegation

Delegation is the interface.

You don't operate Beckett. You ask it. It takes the goal, owns the mess in the middle, and comes back with a shipped result it will stand behind. No dashboard, no prompt engineering, no babysitting.

You manage outcomes, the way you would with a colleague. If that sounds like less control, notice it's the exact amount of control you have over every human coworker you already trust.

03 · the difference

What makes a coworker, not a tool.

initiative

Files its own tickets. Speaks up when it smells trouble, without being asked.

accountability

Signed commits, adversarial review, a paper trail you can audit.

memory

Remembers you tomorrow. Your stack, your taste, your grudges.

presence

Lives where the team already talks. Not another tab you forget about.

taste

Has opinions and pushes back. Occasionally wrong, never beige.

04 · the service

A service without the meter.

The old version of this page ended in a pricing table. The new one ends in a git clone. Beckett is open source and self-hosted: the service in Coworker as a Service is what it does for you, not what you owe monthly.

A coworker you host is the only kind that's really yours. That thinking has its own page: why Beckett is federated.
